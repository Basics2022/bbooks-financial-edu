19. Merton’s portfolio problem#

Merton’s portfolio problem deals with the choice of optimal fraction of investment in a risky asset \(\pi_t\) and the consumption \(c_t\).

Assuming that the wealth of a family or an individual can is invested with a fraction \(\pi_t\) in a risky part of the portfolio with expected return \(\mu\) and standard variation \(\sigma_t\), and with a fraction \(1-\pi_t\) in a risk-free asset with expected return \(r_t\) and zero standard deviation, the wealth \(x_t\) of a family or an individual evolves with the SDE

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} d X_t & = r_t (1 - \pi_t ) X_t \, dt + \mu_t \pi_t X_t \, dt - c_t \, dt + \pi_t \sigma_t X_t \, dW_t = \\ & = r_t X_t \, dt + \left( \mu_t - r_t \right) \pi_t X_t \, dt - c_t \, dt + \pi_t \sigma_t X_t \, dW_t \ , \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

i.e. the equation of a geometric Brownian motion with drift, the same equation that can be used to discuss sequence risk in investment, especially dealing with withdrawal.

Optimization problem can be solved using continuous-time reinforcement learning, see as an example Math:Introduction to RL, and Statistics:RL (todo). Optimal solution \(\pi_t^*\), \(c_t^*\) is the fraction invested in the risky asset and consumption that maximises the value function,

\[V(x,t) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \left. \int_{s=t}^{T} e^{- \rho (s-t)} u(c_s) \, ds + e^{-\rho(T-t)} B(T) u(X_T) \right| X_t = x \right] \ ,\]

i.e. the return of the choice defined as the cumulative discounted reward. Reward per unit time is the utility function \(u(c_s)\), \(\rho\) is a personal discount factor that weights present and future rewards, and \(B(T)\) is a bequest function.

For constant expected return and volatility of the assets, and a utility function \(u(x) = \frac{x^{1-\gamma}}{1 - \gamma}\) it’s possible to find an analytical solution with optimal fraction invested in the risky asset

\[\pi^* = \frac{\mu - r}{\gamma \sigma^2} \ .\]

19.1. Recursive relation and Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman relation#

Writing the value function \(V(X_{t+dt}, t+dt)\) and expanding as a Taylor series up to the first order in \(dt\), it’s possible to find a Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation from a recursive relation.

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} V(X_t+dX_t, t+ dt) & = \mathbb{E} \left[ \left. \int_{s=t+dt}^{T} e^{- \rho (s-t-dt)} u(c_s) \, ds + e^{-\rho(T-t-dt)} B(T) u(X_T) \right| X_{t+dt} \right] = \\ \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

19.1.1. Recursive relation#

\[e^{-\rho t } V(X_t , t ) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \left. \int_{s=t }^{T} e^{- \rho s} u(c_s) \, ds + e^{-\rho T} B(T) u(X_T) \right| X_{t } \right]\]
\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} e^{-\rho (t+dt)} V(X_t+dX_t, t+dt) & = \mathbb{E} \left[ \left. \int_{s=t+dt}^{T} e^{- \rho s} u(c_s) \, ds + e^{-\rho T} B(T) u(X_T) \right| X_{t+dt} \right] = \\ & = \mathbb{E} \left[ \left. \int_{s=t }^{T} e^{- \rho s} u(c_s) \, ds + e^{-\rho T} B(T) u(X_T) \right| X_{t+dt} \right] - \mathbb{E} \left[ \left. \int_{s=t}^{t+dt} e^{- \rho s} u(c_s) \, ds \right| X_{t+dt} \right] = \\ \end{aligned}\end{split}\]
\[e^{-\rho (t+dt)} V(X_t+dX_t, t+dt) = e^{-\rho t} V(X_t, t) - e^{-\rho t} u(c_t) \, dt\]
\[d \left( e^{-\rho t} V(X_t, t) \right) = - e^{-\rho t} u(c_t) \, dt\]
\[\rho V \, dt = d V + u(c_t) \, dt \]

19.1.2. Taylor expansion#

Taylor expansion of value function \(v(t,x)\), valued with \(x = X_t\), reads

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} dv & = \partial_t v \, dt + \partial_x v \, dx + \dfrac{1}{2} \partial_{xx} v \, dx^2 + o(dt) = \\ & = \partial_t v \, dt + \partial_x v \, dX_t + \dfrac{1}{2} \partial_{xx} v \, dX_t^2 + o(dt) = \\ & = dt \left( \partial_t v + \partial_x v ( r_t + (\mu_t - r_t) \pi_t) X_t - \partial_x v \, c_t \right) + \partial_x v \, \pi_t \sigma_t X_t \, dW_t + \dfrac{1}{2} \partial_{xx} v \, \left( \pi_t \sigma_t X_t \right)^2 \, dt + o(dt) = \\ \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

as \(dX_t^2 = \left( \pi_t \sigma_t X_t \right)^2 dW_t^2 + o(dt) = \left( \pi_t \sigma_t X_t \right)^2 dt + o(dt)\). Taking expected value,

\[d V = dt \, \mathbb{E}\left[ \partial_t v + \partial_x v ( r_t + (\mu_t - r_t) \pi_t) X_t - \partial_x v \, c_t + \dfrac{1}{2} \partial_{xx} v \, \left( \pi_t \sigma_t X_t \right)^2 \right] \ .\]

19.1.3. Recursive relation for optimal solution#

First order in \(dt\) reads

\[\rho V^* = \max_{\pi_t, c_t} \left\{ \partial_t V^* + \partial_x V^* \left[ (\pi_t (\mu-r) + r) X_t - c_t \right] + \dfrac{1}{2} \partial_{xx} V^* (\pi \sigma_t X_t)^2 + u(c_t) \right\} = \max_{\pi_t, c_t} \Phi\]

19.1.4. Optimality condition#

Zero gradient is a necessary condition for local extreme points,

\[\begin{split}\begin{cases} 0 = \partial_{\pi_t} \Phi(\pi^*_t, c^*_t) = (\mu-r) X_t \partial_x V^* + \partial_{xx} V^* \pi^*_t \left( \sigma_t X_t \right)^2 \\ 0 = \partial_{ c_t} \Phi(\pi^*_t, c^*_t) = - \partial_x V^* + \partial_{c_t} u(c^*_t) \\ \end{cases}\end{split}\]

and thus

\[\begin{split} \pi^*_t = -\dfrac{\partial_x V^*}{\partial_{xx} V^*} \dfrac{\mu - r}{X_t \sigma^2} \\ \end{split}\]

19.1.5. Example: utility function \(u(x) = \frac{x^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}\)#

\[\begin{split}\begin{cases} \pi^*_t = -\dfrac{\partial_x V^*}{\partial_{xx} V^*} \dfrac{\mu - r}{X_t \sigma^2} \\ c^*_t = \left( \partial_x V^* \right)^{-\frac{1}{\gamma}} \end{cases}\end{split}\]

19.1.6. Example: value function \(\ V^*(t, x) = f^{\gamma}(t) \frac{x^{1-\gamma}}{1 - \gamma}\)#

With value function \(V^*(x,t) = \dots\) and bequest function \(B(T) = \varepsilon^T\)

\[\dot{f}(t) = \nu \, f(t) - 1\]

with final condition \(f(T) = \varepsilon\), and

\[\nu = \dfrac{1}{\gamma} \left\{ \rho - (1 - \gamma) \left( \dfrac{(\mu-r)^2}{2 \sigma^2 \gamma} + r \right) \right\} \ .\]

The solution of the ODE reads

\[\begin{split}f(t) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{1}{\nu} + \left( \varepsilon - \dfrac{1}{\nu} \right) e^{-\nu (T-t)} & \text{,} \quad \text{ if $\nu \ne 0$} \\ T - t + \varepsilon & \text{,} \quad \text{ if $\nu = 0$} \\ \end{cases}\end{split}\]

Thus

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} \pi^*_t & = \dfrac{\mu - r}{\gamma \sigma^2} \\ c^*_t & = \dfrac{X_t}{f(t)} \\ \end{aligned}\end{split}\]
